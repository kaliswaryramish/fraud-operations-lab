# Professional Profile

## The Problems I Solve

Fraud and financial crime operations rarely fail because of a single bad control — they fail because of gaps between teams: between what Product ships, what Policy intends, what Engineering builds, and what an investigator can actually action in the time they have. Most of my work has been about closing those gaps.

Day to day, that means:

- Investigating fraud cases across payments, banking, and chargebacks
- Assessing merchant risk and identifying emerging financial crime typologies
- Investigating account takeover cases as part of regular casework
- Liaising directly with law enforcement (including the Singapore Police Force) on scam tracing and escalation
- Building the SQL queries and dashboards that turn raw case data into something a team can act on
- Redesigning workflows (Microsoft Dynamics 365, case management tooling) so operations teams aren't fighting duplicate processes

## How I Approach Fraud Investigations

I treat every investigation as having two audiences: the immediate one (was this transaction/account/merchant fraudulent, and what's the right action) and the longer-term one (what does this case tell us about a control gap, and how do we stop the next hundred like it). That second question is usually where the more durable work happens — root cause analysis that feeds back into control design rather than one-off case resolution.

I've spent a meaningful part of my career on the tooling side of this: building automated investigation workflows, standing up management information (MI) reporting so leadership can see risk trends in near real time, and more recently working with AI-assisted investigation tooling to reduce manual handling time without cutting corners on evidence quality or decision consistency.

## Operational Style

- **Cross-functional by necessity**: fraud controls that only make sense to the fraud team don't survive contact with Product or Engineering roadmaps. I work directly with Product, Data Analytics, Engineering, and Policy to keep controls both effective and implementable.
- **Regional, not single-market**: my casework and process design span five Southeast Asian markets, which means accounting for regulatory and operational variance rather than designing for one market and hoping it generalizes.
- **Documentation-first**: SOPs, knowledge platforms, and training materials aren't an afterthought — they're how consistency survives team turnover and regional scale.
